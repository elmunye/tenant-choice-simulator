01

How it works

The simulator answers questions of the form: given a target tenant segment and a comp set, what share of that segment will choose your property, and how does that share respond to changes in amenities or rent?

It does this in four steps.

1
Construct synthetic tenant personas
Renter personas are generated to reflect realistic combinations of demographics, lifestyle, and budget. Each persona behaves as one synthetic respondent in a conjoint-style choice study.
2
Run a choice experiment
Personas are presented with many property configurations that vary on rent, unit size, and amenities. For each comparison, the persona picks a preferred configuration or opts out entirely.
3
Estimate utilities and willingness to pay
A standard multinomial logit model is fit to the choices. The output is a utility score for each amenity and rent level, which can be expressed in dollar terms as willingness to pay per amenity.
4
Simulate market share against a comp set
For any property and any set of comps you define, the model projects what share of each tenant segment would choose your property. Adjusting amenities, rent, or the persona mix updates the projection in real time.

Why this approach

Conjoint analysis is the standard method for recovering preferences from indirect choices. It is used widely in consumer goods, automotive, and pharma, and is more reliable than asking respondents to state willingness to pay directly. Using synthetic respondents instead of real ones is a more recent extension of the method, made possible by large language models that can simulate plausible choice behavior at low cost.

02

Research the model draws on

The approach used here is grounded in two recent studies that evaluated synthetic respondents against real human survey data at large scale. Both are openly available and both are explicit about what this method can and cannot do.

Microsoft · Harvard Business School · 2026
Using LLMs for Market Research
Brand, J., Israeli, A., Ngwe, D.
The authors ran parallel human and synthetic conjoint studies across toothpaste, laptops, and tablets. Out of the box, synthetic respondents produced willingness-to-pay estimates that were directionally correct for many attributes but unreliable for others, with wrong signs and 2 to 3 times magnitude errors in some cases. Fine-tuning the model on real survey data from the same product category substantially improved alignment, including for new product features not seen during training. The paper concludes that synthetic respondents are best used as a complement to human surveys, not a substitute.
Ipsos · 2024
The Viability of Large Language Models for Conjoint and MaxDiff Analysis
Ipsos Data Science and Advanced Analytics
One of the largest studies in this area, with 250,000 synthetic responses across five commercial studies. The Ipsos team tested multiple LLMs and prompt strategies, benchmarked against a sample of 500 real respondents. Their findings align closely with the Brand et al. work: synthetic respondents can identify the most and least valued options reasonably well, but tend to overestimate magnitudes, struggle with non-ordered categorical attributes, and show limited differentiation across personas. Training the model on real responses from a separate sample produced meaningful improvements on every dimension they measured.

Both papers are referenced in the citations on the landing page and are recommended reading for any user evaluating the model for a high-stakes decision.

03

Known limitations

The simulator inherits the limitations documented in the research. These are not hypothetical caveats; they are specific behaviors that have been observed and quantified.

Document this when presenting results
  • Wrong-signed estimates can occur. For some amenities, the model can predict positive willingness to pay where humans show negative, or vice versa. This is rare but possible and is most likely on amenities that are unusual or have little discussion in publicly available text.
  • Magnitudes can be off by 2 to 3 times. Even when the direction is right, the dollar value of an amenity may be substantially overestimated or underestimated relative to what a real survey would produce.
  • Demographic differentiation is weak. Differences in preferences across income brackets, age groups, or other segments are recovered less reliably than population-average preferences. Segment-level reads should be treated as directional, not precise.
  • Results reflect a static snapshot. The underlying model was pre-trained on text from a fixed period. It does not reflect very recent shifts in tenant behavior, local market conditions, or features that have only emerged in the last year.
  • This product has not been fine-tuned on real tenant data. The strongest result from both research papers is that fine-tuning on real human survey data substantially improves accuracy. We have not yet done this for multifamily; doing so is the most direct path to better results.

The right way to use the simulator, given these limitations, is as a fast first pass: to narrow options, generate hypotheses, and pressure-test assumptions before committing capital or commissioning a full market study. It is not a replacement for real-respondent research on decisions where the dollar stakes warrant it.

04

Validating the results

The most direct way to improve the reliability of any specific simulation is to fine-tune or calibrate the model with real human responses from the target market. This is not a feature of the current product; it is the recommended next step for users whose decisions require defensible numbers.

There are two practical paths:

Validation survey

A focused real-respondent conjoint study, typically 200 to 400 tenants in the target market, run through a panel provider or a market research firm. The results can be compared directly to the synthetic estimates to identify where the model is reliable and where it drifts. For high-stakes underwriting or lender review, this is the standard practice.

Fine-tuning

The choices collected from a real-respondent survey can also be used to fine-tune the underlying model. Both research papers cited above show that this substantially improves alignment, including for amenities and configurations not directly tested in the survey. Once fine-tuned for a given market or segment, the model produces meaningfully more reliable estimates for that context.

Recommended next step

For any decision where the dollar stakes warrant it, plan a real-respondent validation survey before relying on the simulator's outputs in a committee setting. The synthetic estimates are useful for narrowing the question; the validation survey is what makes the answer defensible.

Results are directional estimates from a synthetic-data simulation. Not a substitute for real-respondent market research on high-stakes decisions.
